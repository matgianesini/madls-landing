Dear Reader

This documentary is an immersive experience centered around the stories of ten women who have lived and survived gender-based violence in Mexico. Divided into chapters, we delve into the very fabric of their experiences, examining the influential factors that shaped their paths and fueled their unwavering resilience.

These stories reverberate with urgency against the backdrop of systemic corruption, the unrelenting challenges posed by COVID-19, and distressingly high rates of gender-based violence. Crafted in collaboration with these brave women themselves, our documentary dares to shatter preconceptions, evoking radical empathy, fostering visibility, and igniting a powerful sense of empowerment.

In Mexico,

the struggle against gender violence has a deep-rooted historical significance. With over 11 women murdered each day, the legal system has demonstrated its incapacity to effectively address this issue. The normalization of violence has left women profoundly vulnerable, with perpetrators rarely held accountable.

A tireless feminist movement has been building for decades, culminating in a pivotal moment on March 8, 2020. More than 80,000 women took to the streets on that day, protesting against gender-based violence and structural machismo. However, just as this movement surged forward, a new crisis emerged, casting a shadow over their progress: the COVID-19 pandemic.

Austerity measures implemented during the pandemic disproportionately affected family caregivers, who are predominantly women, burdening them with increased care work and making access to justice and resources more difficult. This resulted in an unprecedented epidemic of gender-based violence in 2020.

These are the stories of ten women from different parts of Mexico. Their testimonies share their experiences of gender-based violence and resilience, from their childhood to the present. Despite the challenges, these women refuse to be silenced and fight for a better future for themselves and their communities.

Gender-based violence is influenced by power structures and norms, extending beyond individual pathologies. In this chapter, women's personal narratives illuminate how cultural and institutional practices within the family perpetuate and normalize such violence. These stories provide a deeper understanding of the enduring impact of a violent upbringing on individuals' encounters with gender-based violence in adulthood. Early exposure to violence engenders profound psychological effects, leading to enduring sentiments of shame, guilt, and powerlessness. These consequences heighten vulnerability to future abusive behaviors, impair the women’s capacity to recognize and respond to abuse and erect formidable barriers to seeking assistance and support. The narratives underscore four recurrent themes: the role of religious education in perpetuating victim-blaming, the repercussions of family fragmentation and alcoholism, the socialization process surrounding gender roles, and the disconcerting normalization of violence within society.

RELIGION

Religion's impact on women's lives is complex. While some find strength and solidarity in their faith, others grapple with conflicting expectations. It can both empower and impede efforts to combat gender-based violence. Religious teachings and socialization deeply influence women's perspectives, perpetuating stereotypes and normalizing misogyny, leading to feelings of guilt and self-blame regarding violence. Traditional interpretations of religious doctrines often affirm male authority and subordinate women. These restrictions, coupled with limited education, result in self-blame, lack of awareness about violence, and reluctance to seek help.Ultimately, religion plays a foundational role in shaping women's lives and, in many instances, contributes to the prevalence of gender-based violence.

NORMALIZATION OF VIOLENCE

Violence during childhood leaves enduring marks on women's lives, shaping their trajectories. Those who experience violence as children may become perpetrators or face a higher risk of victimization as adults. The normalization of violence hampers women's ability to recognize and heal from their trauma, impacting their self-image and relationships, and leading to struggles with self-esteem and boundary establishment. Distorted notions of "normal" relationships often perpetuate cycles of mistreatment. Additionally, marginalized women face compounded challenges due to the intersection of childhood gender-based violence and other forms of discrimination, limiting their agency and well-being. Different cultural contexts play a pivotal role, often reinforcing oppressive gender dynamics through societal beliefs, practices, and norms that silence women and discourage seeking help or justice.

ALCOHOLISM

Alcoholism often fuels childhood violence and has a profound impact on women's lives. Women describe how alcoholism fosters a culture of male dominance, normalizing aggression and control, while societal norms view violence as a manifestation of masculinity. These factors contribute to violence in certain communities. Alcoholism strains family relationships, ultimately leading to dysfunctional and fragmented families. Behaviors associated with alcoholism, such as physical and emotional abuse, neglect, financial instability, and unpredictable mood swings, destroy trust and create a hostile environment. Children, particularly girls, are highly vulnerable and endure fear, anxiety, and insecurity due to the unpredictable actions of their alcoholic parent. Families affected by alcoholism face immense stress, conflicts, and breakdowns in communication, often resulting in distancing, seeking refuge elsewhere, or cutting ties.

GENDER ROLE SOCIALIZATION

The prevalence of gender norms in childhood significantly impacts women in adulthood, shaping their career choices, self-perception, relationships, and overall well-being. These norms limit career opportunities and reinforce traditional roles, leading to occupational segregation. They also contribute to body image issues and low self-confidence due to societal beauty standards. In relationships, women face expectations of caregiving and emotional labor, while gender-based violence and limited autonomy persist.

This chapter delves into how cultural and institutional practices perpetuate gender-based violence within intimate partnerships. It explores themes that encompass the impact of women's childhood experiences and the challenges they face later in life. The chapter emphasizes the interplay of cultural norms, economic factors, social hierarchies, and psychological mechanisms affecting women, with a particular focus on themes of power and control. Additionally, it highlights the profound impact of violence on self-esteem while examining potential empowering catalysts, such as support from caring bystanders or a protective instinct for their children, which can help women escape from abusive partnerships.

BYSTANDERS

Women have underscored the vital role that bystanders can play, influencing their ability, or lack thereof, to leave such harmful situations. Cultural factors, community dynamics, and social norms often contribute to viewing violence against women within partnerships as normal, commonplace, and a private matter, discouraging intervention. Bystanders may choose inaction, leading to a diffusion of responsibility, or they might actively perpetuate the violence and exacerbate suffering by pressuring the victim to remain with the aggressor due to social and cultural pressures. However, there have been instances where active bystanders have brought about positive and transformative change. Empowering women to prioritize themselves and facilitating their exit requires believing in them, offering emotional support, and providing safe havens for both their physical and emotional well-being.

JEALOUSY

In the context of abusive partnerships, jealousy assumes a profound and influential role, tightly bound to reinforced gender roles. Men, conditioned to perceive women as possessions, find cultural norms justifying possessiveness as a form of control. When coupled with substance abuse, jealousy often escalates into violent behaviors, further exacerbating the situation. Jealousy is often used as a manipulative tool, isolating victims and eroding their autonomy while establishing dominance over their actions and relationships. This emotion becomes a crucial player in the repetitive cycle of abuse, first simmering during the tension-building phase and eventually reaching an explosive eruption of violence, followed by a remorseful or honeymoon period. The emotional entanglement of jealousy with various elements creates a daunting landscape for victims, ensnaring them in a distressing cycle of vulnerability and anguish.

RELIGION

In this intricate landscape of religious beliefs, women's experiences can be shaped by both empowerment and entrapment, profoundly impacting their journey toward healing and liberation.
Teachings that emphasize submission and obedience can burden women with unwarranted guilt and self-blame, convincing them that they bear responsibility for the abuse they live. The pervasive influence of Marianismo, which idolizes women as self-sacrificing and nurturing, adds further pressure for them to silently endure to uphold family unity and a virtuous image before society. The notions of forgiveness and endurance is also often encouraged, and certain religious communities discourage divorce or separation, leaving women feeling morally bound to persevere through ongoing abuse. Although some find comfort and support within their religious communities, others face judgment and dismissal when revealing their abuse, leading to isolation and reluctance to seek help.

CHILDREN

The involvement of children in abusive partnerships is deeply troubling. Women facing such situations highlight two main aspects: the use of children as weapons and their role as lifelines for survival. In abusive relationships or custody battles, one partner may manipulate and control the other by using the children to gain dominance. Conversely, some women feel compelled to stay in abusive relationships due to financial constraints or lack of support, viewing their children as a reason to endure the hardships for their stability and security. However, the ultimate reason most women decide to break free is the realization that their children are in direct danger or suffering, driving them to seek safety and escape the abusive environment.

GENDER ROLES

Gender roles within a partnership can lead to the emergence of violent dynamics through various mechanisms. Firstly, deeply ingrained traditional norms create an uneven distribution of power, with men assuming dominant roles and women adopting submissive positions. As a consequence, women find themselves involved in a domestic partnership, exacerbating their vulnerability and limiting their independence while diminishing ties with external support systems. This isolation often hampers their ability to seek assistance from sources beyond the household. The situation can be further compounded by social stigma, deterring victims from seeking help even when support is available, particularly if such action challenges conventional gender norms. Additionally, economic dependency on male partners poses a significant challenge, trapping women within abusive relationships due to financial constraints that hinder their ability to leave. Abusers often exploit this vulnerability to maintain control over their victims.

HARM TO SELF-PERCEPTION

Gender-based violence in partnerships profoundly impacts self-love and self-esteem. The emotional toll often starts with "Love bombing," creating a powerful bond that obscures warning signs. The abuser alternates between kindness and abuse, fostering a persistent sense of diminished self-perception and worth through invalidation, criticism, and blame. Gaslighting worsens the situation, sowing self-doubt and questioning sanity. Despite mistreatment, victims may seek validation from their abusers, holding onto memories of the initial "Love bombing" and grappling with the complex interplay between affection and harm. This distorted perception can hinder recognizing the abusive nature of the relationship, resulting in learned helplessness over time, where breaking free becomes a formidable challenge. Even after leaving the relationship, women have described how the scars on self-esteem can persist long after the abuse has ended.

This chapter delves into institutional violence within the context of gender-based violence, highlighting its various forms when the criminal justice system fails women– survivors re-victimized, women judged without gender perspective, and those impacted by media sensationalism. These challenges, unforeseen by many women as they strive to extricate themselves from violent relationships, find their roots in a confluence of factors: the scarcity of resources, deeply ingrained institutional misogyny, bureaucratic malfunction, pervasive corruption, and the perils of inaction. While the chapter offers glimpses of instances where government institutions and the media have provided assistance, it critically dissects the roles and privileges of those who manage to maneuver within the system. Simultaneously, it lays bare the harsh reality confronted by the vast majority of women, traversing a path to seek solace in institutional support.

SOCIAL MEDIA

Social media platforms offer rapid information dissemination, presenting both advantages and disadvantages for women's causes. While they enable swift sharing of valuable information, they also harbor potential drawbacks. Harmful stereotypes can be amplified, harassment and cyberbullying find a platform, and victims of crime may face retraumatization. Struggles in effective content moderation persist, with cumbersome reporting mechanisms allowing abusive material to persist. This hampers victim support and raises concerns about the dissemination of sensationalist and unethical reporting. Such coverage fosters harm and disrespect perpetuating violence against women. Hate speech, misogyny, and glorification thrive in these posts, fostering an atmosphere tolerant of gender-based violence. This is similar to issues concerning the proliferation of "revenge porn," which is abetted by social media.These instances yield emotional distress and foster a hostile online realm mirroring real-world gender-based violence.

THE CRIMINAL JUSTICE SYSTEM

The criminal justice system lacks a gender lens, perpetuating gender-based and institutional violence. This issue spans punishment design, defense and prosecution of women, and treatment of incarcerated individuals. The system consistently fails to defend women from gender-based violence due to inadequate investigation, prosecution, and sentencing. Bias against women, whether as victims or perpetrators, results in their experiences being denied, minimized, or blamed, while male aggressors frequently escape consequences. Simultaneously, women accused of crimes encounter swift guilty verdicts and harsher sentences than men. Certain women turn to crime due to self-defense or coercion, yet the system ignores these circumstances, worsening gender-based injustice. Furthermore, insufficient training on gender issues for law enforcement, prosecutors, and judges results in mishandling violence against women and gender-based crimes. Finally, incarcerated women disproportionately suffer due to the neglect of their gender-specific needs, impacting healthcare, safety, and family support.

CORRUPTION

Corruption poses significant barriers for women seeking justice in Mexico. Biased investigations and prosecutions in cases of violence against women often result from corrupt practices, fostering a culture of impunity that discourages reporting of crimes. Moreover, corruption diverts crucial funds from women's rights programs, leaving the judicial system ill-prepared to address women's cases. Judges' decisions may be swayed by corruption, disproportionately favoring male perpetrators, while female victims may face intimidation, further hindering their access to justice. Consequently, a culture of silence and fear is reinforced, dissuading women from pursuing legal remedies.

GOVERNMENT DYSFUNCTION

The inefficiencies within prosecutors' offices contribute to a backdrop of institutional violence that disproportionately impacts women. The criminal justice system's struggles in effectively addressing gender-based violence are driven by several factors, including inadequate resources, overwhelming caseloads concentrated in gender-based violence cases, and corrupt incentives. These elements perpetuate impunity and extend pre-trial detention, magnifying vulnerabilities for women within a challenging system. Furthermore, a culture of victim-blaming and gender stereotypes obstruct empathy for women, shaping case handling and often leading to skepticism and disbelief when reporting crimes. Outdated, insufficiently gender-sensitive training compounds these issues, often leading to re victimizing practices. These systemic deficiencies converge to create an inherently unjust environment for women.

This chapter delves into the transformative journeys of women seeking healing through established institutions and organizations. Their narratives highlight the crucial roles of psychological support, education, support networks, and economic independence. These elements collectively empower women, equipping them with tools for emotional strength, informed decision-making, and recognition of harmful patterns. They foster community, enable autonomy, and lay a stable foundation for recovery, ultimately promoting liberation from violence and resilience. These recurring themes underscore the potential of gender-conscious and accessible institutional practices to drive meaningful change.

SUPPORT GROUPS

Support groups play a crucial role in the recovery, strength, and healing process for individuals in or leaving violent situations. They provide a safe space for connecting with others who have experienced similar circumstances, offering validation and empathy, reducing isolation. Observing others' progress and success stories in the group inspires and gives hope to those overwhelmed by their own recovery journey. Additionally, support groups share coping strategies for challenges resulting from violence, contributing to rebuilding self-esteem, identifying unhealthy patterns, and fostering healthier relationships. Moreover, they challenge the stigma around abuse and mental health, encouraging help-seeking without fear of judgment.

PSYCHOLOGICAL SUPPORT

Psychological support has proven essential for many women, whether they are currently in, leaving, or recovering from violent relationships. It offers a safe space to express emotions and provides coping strategies and safety planning to navigate the challenges of leaving. Moreover, it aids in rebuilding self-esteem, healing from trauma, and recognizing and breaking free from unhealthy patterns. The support empowers individuals to make informed decisions, addresses mental health issues, and strengthens their support network, promoting resilience and preventing revictimization.

EDUCATION

Education and workshops on gender-based violence play a crucial role in empowering women. They provide essential knowledge about their rights, different types of abuse, and available resources. Through these programs, women learn to recognize abusive patterns and break the silence surrounding abuse. The workshops also help women build support networks and offer safety planning strategies to protect themselves in abusive relationships or during the process of leaving. Moreover, education and workshops promote healing from the trauma of abuse and contribute to preventing revictimization. Overall, these initiatives are instrumental in aiding women in abusive relationships, those leaving, or those who have already left, supporting their well-being and fostering resilience.

ECONOMIC INDEPENDENCE

Economic independence is a vital factor in empowering women to leave abusive relationships and rebuild their lives. It not only reduces their reliance on the abuser, easing their exit from such harmful situations, but also grants them access to counseling, legal aid, housing options, and other crucial resources during and after their departure. Financial self-sufficiency fosters a sense of empowerment, enabling women to make choices that align with their best interests and bolster their self-esteem. This newfound independence lays the groundwork for a brighter and more hopeful future post-abuse. Moreover, it sets a positive example for children, highlighting the significance of self-reliance and resilience, and encouraging the next generation to break the cycle of abuse.

In this chapter, we explore the transformative experiences of women who have sought healing beyond conventional institutions and formal organizations. Their narratives reveal the importance of traditional medicine and spiritualism in their healing journeys. Their stories highlight the diverse resilience mechanisms women have discovered within alternative spaces and healing philosophies, fostering holistic approaches, community connections, cultural familiarity, and trust-building. Engaging with traditional medicine, nature, shamanic practices, and family constellations, these women navigate their trauma, reconnect with their bodies, reclaim their narratives, and draw strength from within. These recurring themes illuminate the multifaceted nature of healing practices, underscoring the cultural, symbolic, social, and embodied dimensions that shape women's transformative healing experiences.

NATURE

Fostering and seeking a connection with one's natural surroundings can become a profound catalyst for recovery in the aftermath of gender-based violence. By embracing the natural world, individuals can invite a sense of interconnectedness with their community and surroundings, renewing their sense of belonging. Cultural and spiritual ties to the land provide an avenue to reclaim agency and establish a connection with ancestral heritage. Furthermore, nature's sensory tapestry can lead a person to engage their senses and offer respite from overwhelming experiences associated with trauma. Women have described how immersion in natural environments can help them ground themselves, regulate their emotions and find solace in the beauty of the natural world.

TRADITIONAL MEDICINE

Through their cultural, social, and symbolic dimensions, traditional healing practices offer potential for overcoming trauma caused by gender-based violence . Rooted in cultural meanings and beliefs, these practices incorporate rituals and symbols for healing and transformation within a structured, symbolic space. They provide survivors with validation, support, and a sense of belonging within their communities, fostering resilience and recovery. Additionally, traditional medicine such as Kambô and Yopo, known for their purgative properties, can cleanse the body physically and spiritually. They restore balance, clear energetic blockages, and deepen the connection to oneself and the spiritual realm. Kambô and Yopo can also release emotional trauma, reduce anxiety, and alleviate symptoms of depression. Participants often find catharsis and gain insights into their emotions, relieving psychological burdens. These practices, complemented by sensory experiences, contribute to the overall healing process.

FAMILY CONSTELLATIONS

Family constellations offer a transformative approach for women healing from gender-based violence. By exploring systemic dynamics, these therapeutic sessions illuminate intergenerational influences and ancestral resonances. Through representation and guided facilitation, women confront generational wounds, reclaim personal power, and establish healthy boundaries. Facilitated by trained professionals, family constellations provide a supportive environment for resolution, integration, and narrative transformation. This process breaks the cycle of violence, fosters supportive connections, and restores a sense of safety, strength, and belonging.

SHAMANISM

Shamanism in Mexico is a culturally embedded tradition for healing and transformation, especially in trauma contexts. Rooted in indigenous practices, it restores harmony through techniques like soul retrieval, energy healing, and cleanses (limpias). Rituals and ceremonies empower individuals to release pain and self-blame, supported by the community and shamanic healers. The often syncretic nature of Mexican shamanism integrates indigenous and Catholic elements, respecting cultural and spiritual identities. Incorporating Catholic symbols and prayers into personal rituals honors this holistic approach.

SPIRITUALISM AND FAITH

Spiritualism and faith play an integral role in the healing trajectories of many women, offering solace, meaning, and transformative experiences. Engaging with their beliefs allows survivors of gender-based violence to discover comfort and purpose. Within faith communities, communal support thrives, as rituals and ceremonies provide a platform for collective empathy, understanding, and empowerment. These concepts intersect with liminality and communitas, signifying milestones in the healing journey and nurturing resilience. By embracing faith and spirituality, survivors not only cultivate self-compassion but also address existential questions and find guidance in their path to healing.

This chapter delves into the nuanced and highly individualized healing processes of women, which not only facilitate personal growth and well-being but also manifest as catalysts for collective healing and transformative change within society. Through engagement with artistic expression, activism, feminism, and mutual support, women navigate a trajectory of healing that extends beyond their own experiences, allowing them to contribute significantly to a broader movement aimed at challenging the pervasive normalization of gender-based violence. By serving as a mirror to fellow women, engaging in awareness-raising endeavors, reshaping societal attitudes, and advocating for policy reforms, survivors not only continue their personal healing journeys but also assume a pivotal role in the fundamental reconfiguration of societal norms, fostering an ethos characterized by gender equality and mutual respect.

EMPATHY AND MUTUAL SUPPORT

Women underscore the indispensable role of empathy and reciprocal support in their journey toward healing, serving as both beneficiaries and providers. The act of validating experiences wields a profound influence, countering the doubts and isolation stemming from trauma. This validation not only encourages women to listen to their inner voices but also propels them to proactively pursue their well-being. Supportive individuals establish connections that uplift survivors by accompanying them through healing processes. Post-transformation, many women aspire to reciprocate the support they received, a process that not only aids their own healing but also engenders purposeful and positive action. This empowerment facilitates survivors' reclamation of agency, catalyzing the cessation of traumatic cycles. This evolution from victim to survivor involves a narrative metamorphosis, wherein survivors redefine their roles by bolstering others, thereby fostering individual growth and healing.

FEMINISM AND GENDER EQUITY

Feminism or Gender Equity, as both a dynamic movement and a transformative shift in mindset, plays a pivotal role in women's recovery from gender-based violence and in shaping a resilient future for generations to come. By instilling self-worth and agency, feminism empowers survivors to reclaim control and pursue healing. Within feminist communities, survivors find support and collective healing, while feminist theories challenge gender norms. Addressing foundational underpinnings like power dynamics and toxic masculinity, feminism endeavors to unshackle society from sexist predispositions, while actively laboring to forestall prospective violence. Through resilience, coping strategies, and advocacy, survivors and their allies become agents of change, passing on vital insights. This interaction fortifies feminism's principles, guiding the movement with lived experiences. In essence, feminism bridges recovery, systemic change, and intergenerational resilience.

ARTISTIC PRACTICE AND CREATIVITY

Artistic practice can be an essential source of empowerment for women healing from gender-based violence, providing a secure platform for expression, agency reclamation, and awareness building. Through art, survivors can convey complex emotions, using movement and symbolism to externalize pain and anger, reclaiming control over their narratives and identity. Art can also foster community among survivors through collaborative projects and performances, while amplifying voices challenging societal norms, fostering empathy, and driving action. These efforts contribute to education, prevention, and advocacy, inspiring others to speak out and seek help, creating a ripple of empowerment and change.

ACTIVISM AND PROTEST

Engaging in activism and protest empowers survivors to turn pain into action, transforming personal struggles into a collective force for change. It not only fosters community and solidarity, but it connects women with shared experiences to counter isolation and shame. Through public demonstrations and story-sharing, survivors reclaim agency and empowerment stripped by violence. Activism raises awareness, educating both the public and survivors about the systemic issue of gender-based violence. It empowers survivors to advocate for policy changes and support services, contributing to legal reforms that protect women's rights. By challenging harmful norms, activism promotes gender equality and respect, driving social, cultural, and legal shifts.

ABOUT OUT OF THE SHADOWS...

"Out of the Shadows" is an interactive documentary that delves into the lives of ten resilient women in Mexico, revealing a collective narrative of gender-based violence. Amidst rising violence and diminishing resources, these stories gain significant relevance. By documenting diverse adversities, the documentary explores the intricate interplay of socio-economic, environmental, and political factors fueling violence against women, while highlighting their empowering resilience. Ultimately, the documentary establishes how these experiences are universally relatable to all women––that no woman, no matter their background or position, is invulnerable.

This collaborative methodologies project ensures active participation of the women involved. The protagonists themselves lead the interviews, guiding the stories they wish to share. They have the freedom to choose their level of anonymity, select interview locations, and engage in the editing process, structuring their narratives alongside others. The women become our collaborative storytellers, empowering themselves through visibility, and this documentary is their platform for their voices.

Supported by a Fulbright Scholarship and other funders, this project has collaborated with the Center for Research and Higher Studies in Social Anthropology and local organizations dedicated to gender equity in Mexico. Working alongside survivors of gender-based violence, it aims to create meaningful change: the aim is to raise awareness, promote changes in society and provide support through stories to all affected women.

ROUTE

Biographies

Alex:

Alex, a Mexican-American woman from Mexico City and mother of four, has suffered economic and psychological violence at the hands of her ex-partner. She faces the effects of vicarious violence and a corrupt judicial system that has denied her access to her children for three years. As co-founder of the Front Against Vicarious Violence, she supports women and advocates for laws that recognize this form of gender-based violence. Her work has inspired change in other Latin American countries, paving the way for progress throughout the region.

Bárbara:

Barbara (41), a mother of two from Mexico City escaped the abuses of her powerful notary ex-husband. For six years, she has fought legally against institutional violence and police corruption. Now, her case is in the hands of Congress and she has a strong legal team. Her dedication to justice and love for her children fuel her struggle and inspire those around her.

Betty:

Betty (55), a mother of three in Mexico City, suffered economic and psychological abuse before being imprisoned for her involvement in selling drugs, a desperate measure to pay off debts owed by her ex-partner. During that time, she faced threats from drug traffickers in her community. During her trial and in the Santa Marta and Islas Marias penitentiaries, she suffered institutional abuse in the prison system. Now, as co-founder of Mujeres Unidas por la Libertad, she uses her experience to support other women still fighting for justice within the prison system.

Gabina:

Gabina (33), an indigenous woman from San Esteban Atatlahuca in the Oaxacan Mixteca, has overcome physical and sexual abuse by her brother and economic and psychological abuse in her marriage. She has found solace in traditional medicines such as Kambô and Yopo in her healing process. Inspired by her own experience, Gabina is an advocate against gender violence. Today, she organizes workshops in different municipalities to empower women and address this severe problem in her community, which has been heightened by an agrarian conflict in recent years.

Itzel:

Itzel (34), an elementary school teacher and single mother from the Isthmus of Tehuantepec in Oaxaca, has overcome the challenges of domestic violence, including attempted femicide. Through personal healing, psychology, and activism, she inspires and supports other women in abusive situations. She raises awareness about gender-based violence and empowers women to reclaim their lives. In her classes, she promotes empathy and understanding using a gender lens.

Jennifer:

Jennifer (40), a mother of two from Mexico City, suffered psychological and economic violence during her marriage. After separating, her husband took the children without her consent, plunging her into a painful struggle against "Vicarious Violence". She sought psychological, legal and spiritual help, and became an activist with the National Front Against Vicarious Violence. After institutional challenges, Jennifer was able to get her children back. Together, they are working to rebuild their relationship and overcome the effects of the separation.

Luciana:

Luciana, a visual artist from Mexico City and mother of two, has overcome psychological and economic abuse and attempted incarceration by her ex-partner. Navigating a demanding legal struggle, she finds solace in therapy, meditation, dance, and her art – a dual-purpose endeavor. Her creative work not only raises awareness about gender-based violence and women's experiences but also fuels her personal healing and the inspiration of others.

Monica:

Monica (42), a mother of three children and a domestic worker in Mexico City, managed to overcome seven years of domestic violence by her ex-partner. During that difficult period, her ex-partner denied her the right to pursue her dream of returning to university. However, Monica remained strong and determined, and now aspires to resume her university education. Her courage and determination are reflected in her goal to break the cycle of abuse by passing on feminist principles to her children.

Reina:

Reina, a single mother and an indigenous woman from Oaxaca, has faced institutional violence as a domestic worker in Mexico City and Oaxaca. During the COVID-19 pandemic, she dealt with unemployment, sexual harassment, and economic abuse. In her quest for justice, she has faced institutional obstacles. Inspired by her son's dreams of becoming a doctor, she finds solace in the shamanic practices passed down by her aunt and strength in her Catholic faith. With determination, Reina strives to create a better life for herself and her son.

Rosario

Rosario (45), a woman with indigenous roots in the Oaxacan Mixteca, has overcome various forms of gender-based violence. Since childhood, she has survived physical, sexual, psychological and economic abuse. In her quest for healing, she has participated in educational workshops and received comprehensive support from GESMujer. Inspired by her own transformation, she joined the organization and was named executive director. She leads the team to empower others and foster positive change.

EQUIS JUSTICIA PARA LAS MUJERES

Equis Justice for Women is a dedicated feminist organization advocating for accessible justice for women of diverse backgrounds. Their mission is to dismantle power structures that perpetuate oppression and exclusion. Equis operates within three key domains: preventing and safeguarding against gender-based violence, challenging the unjust criminalization of women, and advocating for impartial, nondiscriminatory justice.

FEMXFEM

Mujeres en Diálogo México, A.C., known as 'FEMXFEM,' is a civil society association established in 2019. Its primary objective is to conduct discussions, training sessions, and experiential activities related to gender violence. Additionally, FEMXFEM offers free legal, psychological, and medical assistance to women who are victims of gender violence.

Contact: femxfem.oficial@gmail.com

NATIONAL FRONT AGAINST VICARIOUS VIOLENCE

The National Front Against Vicarious Violence comprises a collective of women who have endured diverse manifestations of gender-based violence, with a particular emphasis on vicarious violence. Their central objective is to heighten awareness, advocate for legislation, and offer assistance to women impacted by this form of violence. Their dedicated efforts are aimed at driving social transformation, eradicating vicarious violence, and ensuring justice and protection for all affected individuals.

GES MUJER

Ges Mujer is an autonomous civil association dedicated to tackling gender inequality across various domains. Their primary goal is to champion the rights of women and girls through an equity-focused approach. They provide vital services such as psychological and legal counseling, engage in educational endeavors and applied research, curate specialized documentation, drive digital activism, and advance the principles of feminist economics. Operating within both urban and rural areas of Oaxaca, Ges Mujer collaborates extensively with education, health, justice, and municipal authorities.

WOMEN UNITED FOR FREEDOM

Women United for Freedom is an organization established by women who have undergone periods of incarceration. Their central mission revolves around safeguarding human rights both within and beyond the confines of prison. They concentrate their efforts on advancing access to justice, championing rights related to sexual and reproductive health, and advocating for mental health rights. Additionally, they engage in various community-based initiatives to foster employment opportunities and empower women, contributing to the holistic development of the community.
